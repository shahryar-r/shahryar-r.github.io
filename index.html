Generated 3D rotating visualization of the Petrel grid in Python

In reactive-transport simulations of geological CO2 storage, ion activity models control geochemical reactions between rock-forming minerals and CO2-saturated brine. However, the significance of their influence on aqueous speciation and mineral reactions remain uncertain due to the lack of a systematic evaluation. Therefore, I have decided to evaluate the impact of non-ideal ion activity models in relative to the ideal activity model, with the Bunter Sandstone Formation serving as a case study. To do so, I employed TOUGHREACT that its source code was previously extended in Fortran to incorporate ideal activity model. I first converted the Petrel grid of the Bunter Sandstone Formation into VTK format using PyGRDECL to create a 3D rotating visualization of the grid in Python for presentation at 87th EAGE conference. Then, I converted the VTK file of the formation into TOUGH MESH file using toughio for reactive-transport simulations with TOUGHREACT. Six scenarios were developed by combining three initial geochemical equilibrium states with three activity model formulations: ideal, extended Debye-Huckel, and Pitzer. Simulation results indicate that failing to establish the initial geochemical equilibrium before CO2 injection leads to an underestimation of geochemical reactions. In contrast, using an ideal activity model, even after establishing geochemical equilibrium, results in an overestimation of these reactions. Nevertheless, the upper-bound estimates produced by the ideal activity model provide sufficient predictive accuracy for large-scale CO2 storage assessments while significantly reducing computational costs.

Note: The quality of 3D rotating visualization has been decreased to make it suitable for web publishing.

Analyzed inter-simulator sensitivity of CO2 storage predictions

Quantifying the influence of key physical and numerical parameters is essential for improving the reliability of CO2 storage predictions. The Bunter Sandstone Formation is widely regarded as a promising storage candidate in the UK. However, the sensitivity of geochemical reactions to model parameterization remains insufficiently constrained, and reactive-transport simulators can yield divergent predictions. Therefore, I have decided to evaluate the impact of injection rate, lateral boundary condition, grid resolution, and reservoir permeability on reactive-transport simulations of CO2 storage in the Bunter Sandstone Formation, with emphasis on geochemical evolution. To do so, I employed CMG and TOUGHREACT to assess inter-simulator variability in storage predictions under consistent modelling assumptions. The automation pipelines, developed before, were used to process simulation data, followed by Matplotlib, a Python library, for 2D plotting. Despite efforts to ensure methodological consistency, the simulators exhibit systematic but interpretable differences arising from their treatment of relative permeability hysteresis and CO2 dissolution. Nevertheless, both simulators agree that gradually increasing injection rates, compared with constant injection rates, enhances storage capacity while maintaining reservoir pressures below the fracture threshold. Parameter sensitivity analysis also shows that lateral boundary extent and absolute permeability primarily control long-term pressure build-up, whereas grid resolution and relative permeability more strongly influence CO2 plume migration. Grid resolution has the greatest impact on the simulated aqueous geochemistry and mineral reactions, likely due to changes in the numerical representation of CO2 concentration gradients and resulting acidity conditions. Overall, I have concluded that accounting for uncertainty in both physical and numerical parameters is of importance for the reliable CO2 storage assessments in the Bunter Sandstone Formation.

Conducted reactive-transport simulations of geological CO2 storage

Reliable prediction of subsurface CO2 behavior is critical for the safe deployment of geological CO2 storage. The Bunter Sandstone Formation is widely recognized as a promising storage candidate in the UK. However, uncertainties remain regarding geochemical reactions between rock-forming minerals and CO2-saturated brine and the consistency of storage predictions across reactive-transport simulators. Therefore, I have decided to investigate mineral trapping and associated geochemical evolution during CO2 injection in the Bunter Sandstone Formation. To do so, I employed CMG and TOUGHREACT to evaluate inter-simulator variability in storage predictions under harmonized modelling conditions. The automation pipelines, developed before, were used to process simulation data, followed by PyVista, a Python library, for 3D visualization. Despite efforts to ensure methodological consistency, the simulators produce systematic but interpretable differences due to their distinct treatments of relative permeability hysteresis and CO2 dissolution. TOUGHREACT predicts a larger CO2 plume beneath the caprock, while CMG predicts greater pressure build-up near the injection zone. Nevertheless, Calcite dissolution in both simulators indicates acidity perturbations confined to the near-well region, suggesting limited formation-scale geochemical disturbance. The contribution of structural trapping peaks at over 80% at well shut-in, whereas mineral trapping contributes approximately 4% by the end of the simulation period. Overall, I have concluded that it is important to interpret storage predictions within a practical uncertainty envelope defined by inter-simulator outcomes. This end-member scenario may improve confidence in CO2 storage assessments in the Bunter Sandstone Formation.

Developed a workflow for geochemical initialization of aquifers

Ensuring the safe, long-term CO2 storage in geological formations requires accurate modelling of geochemical reactions between CO2-saturated water and rock-forming minerals. Reactive-transport simulators represent these processes over extended timescales, but geochemical equilibrium must first be established, analogous to gravitational equilibrium in pressure initialization. Therefore, I have integrated geochemical modelling with thermodynamic equilibrium constraints to develop a practical workflow for chemically initializing geological formations prior to CO2 injection, leading to a peer-reviewed publication. The workflow uses ion activity models to synthesize equilibrated CO2-rock-water systems for a non-negative degree of freedom while deliberately engineering the mineralogy to ensure model predictions of salinity and pH are broadly consistent with laboratory values. The significant discrepancies between activity-model-based equilibrium concentrations and short-term laboratory values highlight the importance of such a synthesis workflow, as laboratory analyses constrained by short experimental timescales may not reflect true long-term equilibrium conditions.

import numpy as np
from datetime import datetime


def import_cmg_data(cell_number, cmg_txt_path):
    nx, ny, nz = cell_number        
    blocks = {}
    current_block = {}
    current_time = None
    first_variable_name = None
    
    i = 0
    j = 0
    k = 0

    skip_until_k = True
    
    if not cmg_txt_path:
        print("dataset not provided")
        return None
        
    else:
        with open(cmg_txt_path, 'r') as f:
            for line in f:
                line = line.strip()
                if not line:
                    continue
                if 'TIME =' in line:
                    skip_until_k = True
                    parts = line.split()
                    if current_time is None:
                        first_timestep = parts[4]
                    difference = datetime.strptime(parts[4], '%Y-%b-%d').year - datetime.strptime(first_timestep, '%Y-%b-%d').year
                    current_time = round(float(difference))
                    if current_time not in blocks:
                        blocks[current_time] = {}
                    continue
                elif 'RESULTS PROP' in line and 'Units:' in line:
                    start = line.find('RESULTS PROP') + len('RESULTS PROP')
                    end = line.find('Units', start)
                    variable_name = line[start:end].strip()
                    if first_variable_name is None:
                        first_variable_name = variable_name
                    continue
                elif 'K =' in line:
                    skip_until_k = False
                    parts = line.replace("**", "").replace(" ", "").split(",")
                    k = int(parts[0].split("=")[1])
                    j = int(parts[1].split("=")[1])
                    i = 1
                    continue
                elif not skip_until_k:
                    parts = line.split()
                    if 'index' not in blocks[current_time]:
                        blocks[current_time]['index'] = []
                    if variable_name not in blocks[current_time]:
                        blocks[current_time][variable_name] = []
                    for value in parts:
                        if variable_name == first_variable_name:
                            blocks[current_time]['index'].append((i, j, k))
                        blocks[current_time][variable_name].append(float(value))
                        i += 1
            
        for timestep, block in blocks.items():
            for name, values in block.items():
                block[name] = np.array(values)

    print('Time steps in cmg file:', list(blocks.keys()))
    print('Variables in cmg file:', list(next(iter(blocks.values())).keys()))
    print('===================================================================')
    return blocks

import numpy as np


def import_toughreact_data(cell_number, *address):
    flow_tec_path = address[0]
    
    if len(address) > 1:
        mineral_tec_path = address[1] 
    else:
        mineral_tec_path = None
    
    if len(address) > 2:
        concentration_tec_path = address[2] 
    else:
        concentration_tec_path = None

    nx, ny, nz = cell_number
    
    def _read_tec(filepath):
        
        blocks = {}
        current_block = {}
        current_time = None
        var_names = []
        with open(filepath, 'r') as f:
            for line in f:
                line = line.strip()
                if not line:
                    continue
                if 'Variables=' in line:
                    # extract variable names in quotes
                    parts = line.replace('Variables=', '').split('"')
                    for i in parts:
                        if i and not i.isspace():
                            var_names.append(i)
                elif 'Zone T=' in line:
                    # save previous block
                    if current_time is not None and current_block:
                        blocks[current_time] = current_block
                    # reset
                    current_block = {}
                    for name in var_names:
                        current_block[name] = []    
                    # parse time between T=" and " sec
                    start = line.find('Zone T=') + len('Zone T=')
                    end = line.find('sec', start)
                    t = line[start:end].strip().strip('"')
                    # handle zero-time special-case
                    if t == '1.00000000E+00':
                        t = '0.00000000E+00'
                    current_time = round(float(t)/3.15360000E+07)
                else:
                    # data lines
                    parts = line.split()
                    if current_time is not None and len(parts) == len(var_names):
                        for i, name in enumerate(var_names):
                            current_block[name].append(float(parts[i]))
        # final block
        if current_time is not None and current_block:
            blocks[current_time] = current_block
        # reshape and flatten
        for timestep, block in blocks.items():
            for name, values in block.items():
                arr = np.array(values).reshape((nx, ny, nz), order='C')
                block[name] = arr.flatten(order='F')
        return blocks

    flow_blocks = _read_tec(flow_tec_path)
    blocks = flow_blocks
    
    if mineral_tec_path:
        mineral_blocks = _read_tec(mineral_tec_path)
        for timestep in mineral_blocks:
            if timestep in blocks:
                blocks[timestep].update(mineral_blocks[timestep])
            else:
                blocks[timestep] = mineral_blocks[timestep]
    else:
        mineral_blocks = None
        print("Mineral file not provided")

    if concentration_tec_path:
        concentration_blocks = _read_tec(concentration_tec_path)
        for timestep in concentration_blocks:
            if timestep in blocks:
                blocks[timestep].update(concentration_blocks[timestep])
            else:
                blocks[timestep] = concentration_blocks[timestep]
    else:
        concentration_blocks = None
        print("Concentration file not provided")

    
    
    print('Time steps in tough file:', list(blocks.keys()))
    print('Variables in tough file:', list(next(iter(blocks.values())).keys()))
    print('===================================================================')
  
    return blocks

Reactive-transport modelling of CO2 storage using TOUGHREACT and CMG requires the visualization of simulation results through a single package for consistency in comparison. The first step in the post-processing visualization is to read the raw output files of simulators line by line, including data loading, character-level parsing, and data structuring. As simulation outcomes are large datasets, this workflow cannot be executed manually. Therefore, I developed Python pipelines, import_cmg_data() and import_toughreact_data(), to automate these data processing tasks. These automation workflows are later employed for 2D and 3D visualization to support consistent analysis across simulation cases.

TOUGHREACT is a non-isothermal, multi-component, multi-phase reactive-transport simulator that can be used for the simulation of subsurface hydrological and biogeochemical environments, including geothermal systems, geological CO2 storage, and environmental remediation. The simulator mathematically models both batch reaction and 3D reactive flow using different ion activity models. Its reactive module includes many geochemical processes, particularly mineral precipitation and dissolution under equilibrium and/or kinetic constraints. However, TOUGHREACT does not natively involve the ideal activity model for geochemical reactions and support tabular or functional input for relative permeability. To overcome these limitations, its Fortran source code was modified through subroutines t2f_v4.f for relative permeability and geochem_v4.f for activity model in TOUGHREACT version 4.13. This modification enables aligning its output with CMG-GEM that is a commercial simulator for compositional, chemical, and unconventional reservoir modelling, even though it does not support batch reaction, and its source code is not available for any modification.

Created GIS-based maps for the potential CO2 storage sites

The first step in evaluating any CO2 storage project is to identify storage systems capable of securely containing CO2 over extended timescales. Among the available options, geological storage remains the most technically and economically viable because of the proven long-term containment of natural hydrocarbons in subsurface formations, the abundance of geological data from oil and gas exploration, and the potential for reusing existing infrastructure. Following the development of a four-stage site-selection workflow, I gathered publicly accessible GIS-based datasets to basically evaluate potential CO2 storage sites across the UK, leading to spatial distribution maps later presented in 3rd International Conference on Energy and Sustainable Futures (ICESF).
